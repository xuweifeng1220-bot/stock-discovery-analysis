# Practitioner-Informed Equity Research Framework

## First Principles

- **AI role**: handle objective information gathering, summarization, valuation arithmetic, technical scanning, and review tables. It should not replace final human judgment.
- **Human role**: define the framework, select high-quality inputs, judge business essence, decide whether the opportunity is within circle of competence, and take responsibility for trades.
- **Core belief**: investment is pricing the future from the present. Returns come from a cognitive gap, not from asking AI to predict a random walk.
- **Market reality**: good industry does not equal good stock. Stock prices often peak before fundamentals fully materialize because markets buy expectations and sell facts.

## Idea Discovery

Prefer opportunities with at least one of these structures:

1. **Ignored long-term certainty**
   - Large industry trend is real, but current attention is low.
   - Example pattern: buy before the market fully recognizes the logic, not after everyone discusses it.

2. **Cost-line or downside-cushion trade**
   - Asset price approaches production cost, replacement value, liquidation value, or extreme pessimism.
   - Then reverse-search for potential catalysts and black-swan upside.

3. **Supply-demand mismatch**
   - Cycle assets should be analyzed through capacity, inventory, demand elasticity, cost curve, and industry discipline.
   - Low PE in a cycle can be a trap near peak earnings.

4. **Narrative option with embedded probability**
   - Technology stocks often price a probability-weighted future: TAM, market share, product success, platform optionality.
   - Analyze what success rate the current valuation implies.

5. **Domestic substitution / policy plus order verification**
   - Policy alone is insufficient.
   - Prefer visible orders, capacity expansion, customer validation, and value-chain beneficiaries.

## Valuation Discipline

Use three scenarios:

- **Pessimistic**: what happens if growth slows, margins normalize, policy support fades, or capital exits?
- **Base**: what current evidence reasonably supports?
- **Optimistic**: what must go right for the bull case to work?

For each scenario, state:

- Revenue or volume driver
- Margin assumption
- Growth duration
- Terminal value or normalized multiple
- Implied market cap / fair value range
- Probability weight
- Key evidence needed to upgrade or downgrade the case

Do not use single-factor valuation:

- **PE** requires growth and cycle context.
- **DCF** requires segment-level assumptions for complex businesses.
- **TAM** requires value capture, competitive position, and timing.
- **Theme heat** requires checking whether valuation has already overdrafted the story.

## Business Archetypes

### Cycle

Focus on supply-demand, capacity cycle, inventory, cost curve, pricing power, policy constraints, and demand elasticity. The best entry often appears near pessimism or cost support, not near peak earnings.

### White Horse / Compounder

Focus on growth durability, moat, pricing power, capital allocation, and whether growth expectations have been reset. Low PE can mean no growth; high PE can be rational only if durability is underpriced.

### Technology / Platform

Break into business segments. Estimate embedded success probability for each future option. Separate real product traction from "faith recharge" and short-lived catalysts.

### Theme / Capital Operation

Check whether the company is truly exposed to the theme or merely borrowing market heat. Be skeptical of concept stocks where business contribution is small, unverifiable, or mainly financial engineering.

### ETF / Macro Asset

Use macro anchors, liquidity, valuation versus GDP/earnings, and structural capital flows. Avoid extrapolating extreme market turnover or index targets without macro arithmetic.

## Technical Analysis Boundary

Use technical analysis for:

- Position sizing
- Entry/exit timing
- Stop-loss tolerance
- Crowding and sentiment check
- Black-swan defense
- Reducing missed alerts from manual monitoring

Do not use it as:

- A replacement for business analysis
- A fully automated trading strategy
- A proof that a thesis is correct

## News and Catalysts

Treat news as verification, not the thesis.

Classify each item:

- **Core evidence**: changes revenue, margin, supply-demand, customer validation, regulation, capital cost, or competitive position.
- **Catalyst**: may accelerate market recognition but does not alter long-term value alone.
- **Noise**: headline without material business impact.
- **Consensus confirmation**: public information that may already be priced in.

Always ask:

- What did the market already know?
- What assumption changed?
- Is this a one-off pulse or durable evidence?
- Who is the real beneficiary in the value chain?

## Black-Swan / Anti-Consensus Thinking

For deep black-swan work, also use `black-swan-template.md`.

Use AI to break consensus expectations:

- Start from a downside-protected or overlooked asset.
- Generate plausible upside surprises and negative shocks.
- Identify which assumptions would have to break.
- Separate "unknown but possible" from "unsupported fantasy."

Output both:

- **Positive black swan**: unexpected benefit, policy shift, supply shock, demand surge, competitor failure.
- **Negative black swan**: valuation collapse, demand destruction, policy reversal, financing stress, margin squeeze.

## Review Logic

Monthly review should emphasize:

- Why did major rising sectors rise?
- Why did major falling sectors fall?
- Which moves were foreseeable from public information?
- Which moves were pure emotion or liquidity?
- Which opportunities were missed because the framework ignored them?
- Which trades were wrong because execution violated the thesis?

For personal trade review, separate:

- **Research correctness**: was the thesis valid?
- **Execution correctness**: was entry, sizing, holding, and exit consistent with the thesis?
- **Emotional error**: did fear, FOMO, anchoring, or outside noise override the plan?

## Default Conclusion Standard

Every stock or sector analysis must end with:

- **Current judgment**: attractive, watchlist, avoid, or insufficient evidence.
- **Why now / why not now**
- **Main upside path**
- **Main downside path**
- **Valuation safety**
- **Catalysts**
- **Invalidation conditions**
- **Next evidence to collect**
