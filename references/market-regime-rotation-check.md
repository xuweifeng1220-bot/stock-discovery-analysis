# Market Regime and Sector Rotation Check

Use this check whenever the user asks whether a stock is worth buying now, whether timing is attractive, whether a sector can outperform, or whether an idea should be prioritized versus other opportunities.

## Core Principle

Capital is limited. Even a valid stock thesis may fail in the short or medium term if market capital is concentrated in a stronger theme, a more liquid sector, or a more urgent narrative.

Every buy-worthiness analysis must answer:

- **What is the current market mainline?**
- **Is the target stock part of it, adjacent to it, waiting for rotation, or being ignored?**
- **If capital is finite, why should money choose this stock now instead of a stronger theme?**

## Step 1: Identify the Current Mainline

List the strongest current market themes or sectors.

| Theme/Sector | Market | Driver | Stage | Evidence | Sustainability |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|

Classify the driver:

- Fundamental earnings upgrade
- Policy support
- Liquidity/rate expectation
- AI/technology narrative
- Commodity or price cycle
- Geopolitical/security theme
- Valuation repair
- Short squeeze or sentiment heat
- Defensive rotation

## Step 2: Locate the Target Stock

Classify the target stock relative to the mainline:

- **Core mainline asset**: direct beneficiary, high liquidity, market focus.
- **Mainline branch**: secondary beneficiary, may rotate after core leaders.
- **Lagging beneficiary**: logic exists but capital has not arrived.
- **Defensive alternative**: attracts capital when risk appetite falls.
- **Competing theme**: fights for the same risk capital.
- **Orphan asset**: thesis may be valid, but no current capital attention.

## Step 3: Rotation Stage

Identify the current stage:

| Stage | Meaning | Buy Timing Implication |
|---|---|---|
| Mainline ignition | New information creates fresh consensus | Best risk-reward if valuation not stretched |
| Acceleration | Leaders rise, capital crowds in | Momentum works, but valuation risk rises |
| High-level diffusion | Money spreads to second/third-tier names | Higher false-positive risk |
| Catch-up rotation | Lagging sectors receive spillover capital | Works only if mainline remains strong |
| Exhaustion | Good news no longer moves prices | Avoid chasing |
| Switch / retreat | Capital leaves old theme for new theme | Prior thesis may be right but timing wrong |

## Step 4: Opportunity Cost

Compare the target stock with stronger alternatives.

| Candidate | Theme Strength | Business Quality | Valuation Safety | Catalyst Visibility | Liquidity | Risk-Reward | Priority |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|---|

Ask:

- Is the target stock better than the current mainline leaders?
- If not, what specific rotation logic supports buying it now?
- Is it cheaper because it is ignored, or cheap because capital has better places to go?
- Is the market rewarding this type of asset in the current regime?

## Step 5: Cross-Market Differences

Use market-specific capital behavior.

### A-Share

Emphasize:

- Policy theme strength
- Retail and hot-money attention
- Daily limit-up diffusion
- Sector rotation speed
- Liquidity and turnover
- Whether the stock is a core ticker or peripheral follower

### Hong Kong

Emphasize:

- Southbound flow
- Valuation repair logic
- Liquidity discount
- China policy expectation
- Earnings certainty
- Whether the theme can overcome structural risk premium

### US

Emphasize:

- Earnings revision
- Rate expectations
- Mega-cap / AI / tech mainline
- Institutional positioning
- Liquidity preference for leaders
- Whether valuation already discounts perfection

## Step 6: Required Conclusion

Any "worth buying now" answer must end with:

- **Current market mainline**
- **Target stock's relationship to the mainline**
- **Stronger competing themes**
- **Capital opportunity cost**
- **Rotation stage**
- **Why now / why not now**
- **Immediate buy, wait for rotation, watchlist, or avoid**

Do not claim a stock is attractive solely because its own fundamentals are good. It must also pass timing, capital preference, and opportunity-cost checks.
